What is Asian America?

The notion of an “Oriental” race is a product of Euro-American colonial imagination.  In fact, the vast diversity of physical characteristics, language, religion, and cultural practice among the peoples designated as “Asian” undermines the very conceit of race as a “natural” category.   Nevertheless, the fiction of race remains the dominant ideology of inequality and domination and central to the American way of life and death.  

The term “Asian American” was invented by Chinese, Japanese and Filipino students during the 1968 Third World Student Strike at San Francisco State College.  “Asian American” expressed unity across disparate lost community histories and solidarity with Asian, African, Latino, and Indigenous peoples fighting for liberation everywhere and rejection of the colonizer’s Oriental..  Over time, however, “Asian American” has been co-opted by the state into yet another category of America’s racial hierarchy, largely divorced from its radical origins. 

Four Things to Know About Asian America

1. More than you think

In 1960, there were fewer than 1 million Asians in America. In 2019, there were 23 million people of Asian descent in the United States.

2. Longer than you think

Most Asian families in the US have arrived since 1965, but Chinese and Filipino sailors, merchants, tradespersons, and servants established a thriving settlement in Mexico City in the 1560s.  In the 1850s, Chinese made up about one-third of California’s population.  

3. From Everywhere to Everywhere

Asian Americans come from 43 countries in Asia and from countries in Africa, Latin America, the Caribbean, and Europe.  Asians live in every state and territory in the U.S., from Guam to Maine.

4. Richer and Poorer

Asian Americans Have The Highest Poverty Rate of Any Racial Group in New York City.  Taiwanese and Indian American families have the highest incomes of any racial or ethnic group in the US. Hmong, Cambodian, Bangladeshi, and Burmese Americans have the lowest incomes of any racial or ethnic groups in the US. Asian Americans have the widest income gaps among all racial groups in the U.S.

Never Your Model Minority

Despite the mass media depictions of Asians as wealthy suburbanites, most Asians in America are urban working-class people, cooks and servers, nurses, cab drivers, nail salon workers, garment workers, clerks, mom-and-pop shopkeepers, and their families. Seventy percent of Asian Americans in college attend two-year community colleges. Since the 1800s, the myth of cheap and docile Asian labor has been a means of controlling the white working class and policing blacks and other people of color.  Nothing could be further from the truth.  In  1867, thousands of striking Chinese railroad workers in the High Sierras demanded equal wages and working conditions with their white counterparts. Since then, Asians have been fighting exploitation, discrimination, and violence in the fields, factories, mines, and dockyards.

Some Dates to Remember

Los Angeles Massacre

1871

After an altercation among Chinese men in which a white man was accidentally shot, a mob of 500 white and Mexican men shot and lynched at least 19 Chinese residents of Los Angeles. 

Page Act

1875

Before the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882, Asian women were effectively barred from entry to the United States by the Page Act based on the assumption that they were being trafficked as sex workers. All Asian women seeking admission were subject to invasive and humiliating interrogations to prove they were “of good moral character.”

Rock Springs Massacre

1885

Welsh, Swedish, Irish, and Cornish immigrant miners under the banner of the Knights of Labor killed at least 28 Chinese coal miners and wounded at least another fifteen. The white mob burned down seventy-eight Chinese houses and drove their residents into the high desert.

Overthrow of the Hawaiian Monarchy

1893

After more than a half-century of political influence and economic dominance during which the indigenous Kanaka-Maoli people were decimated and dispossessed of their land, American planters and merchants organized a coup. They established a “Republic of Hawaii” with the goal of eventual annexation by the US.

Moro Crater Massacre

1903

During the brutal suppression of resistance to the US colonization of the Philippines between 1900 and 1913, US soldiers killed over 1,000 Filipino Muslim men, women, and children in the Bud Dao volcano crater.

Takao Ozawa v. United States, 260 U.S. 178 and United States v. Bhagat Singh Thind, 261 U.S. 204

1922-23

In two successive and somewhat contradictory decisions, the Supreme Court declared persons of Japanese and Indian ancestry, like the Chinese before them, unassimilable and ineligible for naturalization.

Gong Lum v Rice

1927

The US Supreme Court ruled that the American-born daughter of a Chinese immigrant was “colored” and thus subject to Mississippi's segregationist Jim Crow laws.

Executive Order 4099

1941

Franklin Delano Roosevelt subjected 130,000 Japanese Americans living on the West Coast to incarceration without trial for the duration of the war against Japan. Over 100,000 were American citizens, and several thousand others were Japanese immigrants to Latin American countries that the US held for potential exchanges with Japan.

No Gun Ri Massacre

1951

During the Korean War, the US feared there might be infiltrators among the many thousands of uprooted civilians. At No Gun Ri, US soldiers killed 400 Korean men, women, and children fleeing the fighting in their home districts.

My Lai Massacre

1968

In what is known to Vietnamese as the Song My massacre, American soldiers killed between 347 and 504 Vietnamese civilians, men, women, and children in the hamlet of My Lai. American soldiers raped or gang raped at least 38 women and children as young as 13.

Cleveland School Massacre

1989

A 24-year-old white racist who had expressed hatred of Asian immigrants shot and killed five and wounded thirty-two Southeast Asian first and second graders in their schoolyard in Stockton.

Sai-I-Gu

1992

During the uprising touched off by the acquittal of the LAPD officers who beat black motorist Rodney King and the reduced sentence given to the Korean store owner Soon Du-ja in the shooting of Black teenager Latasha Harlins, white, black, and Latino mobs looted and burned down over 2300 Korean-owned stores in Los Angeles.

The Shock and Awe Bombing of Baghdad

2003

The US began its war against Iraq with the massive bombing of Baghdad in March 2003. Over 6,000 Iraqi civilians died in the first two weeks of the war.

Oak Creek Sikh Temple Massacre

2018

A white racist shot and killed six worshippers in the Sikh temple in Oak Creek, Wisconsin. A seventh shooting victim died of his wounds in 2020.
